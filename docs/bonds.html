Bonds

Bonds are fixed income instruments. A zero coupon bond, or bullet has unit cash flow at maturity t. Its price at time 0 is D(t). Its price at time s \le t is D(s, t) so D(t) = D(0,t).

Exercise. Show D(s,t) = D(t)/D(s).

Hint: Replicate a zero maturing at time t by buying 1/D(s,t) of a zero maturing at s then use that at time s to buy a zero at t.

A fixed income instrument has cash flows (c_j) at times (t_j), 0\le j\le n. The issue date is t_0 and maturity is t_n.

For a standard bond with coupon c we have c_0 = 0, c_j = c\delta(t_{j-1}, t_j), 1 < j < n, where \delta is the day count fraction corresponding to the day count basis, and c_n = 1 + c\delta(t_{n-1}, t_n).

At time t the next coupon date t\_(t) = \min\{ t_j \mid t_j > t\} and the previous coupon date \_t(t) = \max\{ t_j \mid t_j \le t\}.

The accrued interest at t is a(t) = c_j \delta(\_t(t), t). The buyer must pay this to the seller since the buyer gets the full coupon at the next coupon date. The dirty price is clean price plus accrued interest. Clean price is the present value of future cash flows.

A fixed income instrument is a portfolio of zero coupon bonds. Its present value at pv date t is p(t) = \sum_{t_j > t} c_j D(t, t_j).

The continuously compounded yield y = y(t) = y(t,p) for price p quoted at t is defined by p = \sum_{t_j > t} c_j e^{-y(t_j - t)}. This is never used in practice and yields involve the compounding frequency n. The n-th annually compounded yield y_n is determined by p = \sum_{t_j > t} c_j(1 + y_n/n)^{-n(t_j - t)}. (Is this correct?)

There are several types of duration. The forward curve f(t) is defined by D(t) = e^{-\int_0^t f(s)\,ds} so D(t,u) = e^{-\int_t^u f(s)\,ds}. Without an adjective, duration is the derivative of price with respect to a paralell shift of the forward curve dp/df = -\sum_{t_j > t} c_j D(t, t_j) (t_j - t), the time weighted average of the discounted cash flows. Typically it is quoted without the minus sign. Macaulay duration is the derivative of the logarithm of the price d(\log p)/df = (dp/df)/p. Modified duration is the derivative of bond price with respect to yield, dp/dy_n = \sum_{t_j > t} c_j(1 + y_n/n)^{-n(t_j - t) - 1}(-n(t_j - t)/n) = -(\sum_{t_j > t} c_j(1 + y_n/n)^{-n(t_j - t)}(t_j - t))/(1 + y_n/n). This is approximately equal to (dp/df)/(1 + y_n/n).

Bond issuers can make bonds cheaper by attaching a call option. The option is specified by a series of times and call prices, the call schedule. At each call date the issuer can pay the holder the call price to terminate their remaining bond cash flow obligations. They can also attach a put option and specify a put schedule allowing the bond holder pay the issuer the put price at any put date to terminate the bond.

Trading Strategy

Given a set of instruments I, trading times T, and information \mathcal{A}_t at t\in T, let X_t\colon\mathcal{A}_t\to\boldsymbol{R}^I be the prices and C_t\colon\mathcal{A}_t\to\boldsymbol{R}^I be the cash flows at time t\in T. A trading strategy is a finite collection of increasing stopping times \tau_j and trades \Gamma_j\colon\mathcal{A}_{\tau_j}\to\boldsymbol{R}^I. Trades accumulate to positions \Delta_t = \sum_{\tau_j < t} \Gamma_j = \sum_{s<t} \Gamma_s where \Gamma_s(\omega) = \Gamma_j(\omega)1(s = \tau_j(\omega)).

The value, or mark-to-market at time t of a trading strategy is V_t = (\Delta_t + \Gamma_t)\cdot X_t. It is the amount unwinding the current position and trades just executed at prevailing market prices would produce.

The account at time t is A_t = \Delta_t\cdot C_t - \Gamma_t\cdot X_t. The trading strategy pays cash flows proportional to the position and trades executed at t must be paid for.
