Arbitrage exists if there is a \gamma^*\in(\boldsymbol{{R}}^I)^* with \gamma^* x < 0 and \gamma^* X \ge0.

Define A\colon (\boldsymbol{{R}}^I)^*\to B(\mathcal{A}_0)\oplus B(\mathcal{A}_1) by \gamma^*\mapsto -\gamma^* x \oplus \gamma^* X.

A^*\colon ba(\mathcal{A}_0)\oplus ba(\mathcal{A}_1)\to\boldsymbol{{R}}^I is defined by \begin{aligned} \langle\gamma^*, A^*(\pi\oplus\Pi)\rangle &= \langle A\gamma^*, \pi\oplus\Pi\rangle \\ &= \langle -\gamma^* x\oplus\gamma^* X, \pi\oplus\Pi\rangle \\ &= \langle -\gamma^* x, \pi\rangle + \langle\gamma^* X, \Pi\rangle \\ &= \langle \gamma^*, \langle -x, \pi\rangle + \langle X, \Pi\rangle\rangle \\ \end{aligned} so A^*(\pi\oplus\Pi) = \langle -x, \pi\rangle + \langle X, \Pi\rangle.

Define the positive cone in ba(\mathcal{A}_0)\oplus ba(\mathcal{A}_1) by \mathcal{P}= \{\pi\oplus\Pi\mid \pi > 0, \Pi\ge0\} Arbitrage exists if and only if the range of A meets \mathcal{P}.

There is no arbitrage if and only if \operatorname{ran}A\cap\mathcal{P}= \emptyset. If so, there exists a hyperplane H\supseteq\operatorname{ran}A with H\cap\mathcal{P}= \emptyset. There exists \pi\oplus\Pi with pre-annihilator {}^\perp\{\pi\oplus\Pi\} = H.

Trinomial American Option

The sample space is the possible stock price movements over the first and second periods, and when the option is exercised. {\Omega = \{(\omega_1, \omega_2, t)\mid \omega_i\in\{-,+\}, t\in\{1,2\}\}}. We write, e.g., -+1 for (-,+,1).

Information at time 0 is \mathcal{A}_0 = \{\Omega\}. We assume \Pi_0(\Omega) = 1. At time one we know if the stock went up or down in the first period and whether or not the option was exercised. so {\mathcal{A}_1 = \{\{--1,-+1\}, \{+-1,++1\}, \{--2,-+2\}, \{+-2,+-2\}\}} We write \Pi_1(--1) = \Pi_1(-+1) = \pi_{-1}, etc. Of course \mathcal{A}_2 consists of all singletons of \Omega. We write \Pi(--1) = \pi_{--1}, etc.

We have X_0(\omega_1,\omega_2, t) = (1, S_0, V_0) where S_0 and V_0 are scalar.

And X_1(\omega_1, \omega_2, t) = (R, S_1(\omega_1), V_1(\omega_1) 1(t = 2)) with C_1 = (0, 0, \nu(S_1(\omega_1)) 1(t = 1)).

Also X_2(\omega_1, \omega_2, t) = (R^2, S_2(\omega_1, \omega_2), 0) with C_2(\omega_1, \omega_2, t) = (0, 0, \nu(S_2(\omega_1, \omega_2))1(t = 2)).

Since X_0\Pi_0 = ((X_1 + C_1)\Pi_1)|_{\mathcal{A}_1} we have \begin{aligned} 1 &= R(\pi_{-1} + \pi_{-2} + \pi_{+1} + \pi_{+2}) \\ S_0 &= S_1(-)(\pi_{-1} + \pi_{-2}) + S_1(+)(\pi_{+1} + \pi_{+2}) \\ V_0 &= S_1(-)\pi_{-2} + S_1(+)\pi_{+2} + \nu(S_1(-))\pi_{-1} + \nu(S_1(+))\pi_{+1} \\ \end{aligned}
