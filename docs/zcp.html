Zero Coupon Bonds

Keith A. Lewis

Finance involves moving cash flows through time. The simplest case is a zero coupon bond that pays one unit at maturity u. Its price is the discount D(u). You can buy it for D(u) today and recieve one unit at u. Modulo bid/ask spread and credit considerations, you can get a cash flow c at time u by paying cD(u) today. Mathematicians like to work with discounts but traders prefer to work in terms of rates. There are various ways of converting rates to discounts. The spot rate r(u) is defined by D(u) = \exp(-ur(u)). The forward rate f(u) if defined by D(u) = \exp(-\int_0^u f(t)\,dt). Neither of these are directly quoted market rates and are called continuously compounded rates based on the mathematical fact e^x = \lim_{n\to\infty}(1 + x/n)^n. They can be defined in terms of discrete compounding periods and day count conventions used in the market.

Realized return is another way to express discount. Instead of a cash flow cD(u) today paying c at maturity u, an investment of c today returns c/D(u) = cR(u) at time u. The realized return is R(u) = 1/D(u).

This is from the perspective of today, but time marches forward. At any time t we have a discount D_t(u) where cD_t(u) invested at time t pays c at time u. The realized return from investing c at time t pays cR_t(u) = c/D_t(u) at time u. If t = 0 represents today then D_0(u) = D(u) and R_0(u) = R(u).

What economists like to call the law of one price can be used to show how the discount curve D_t(u) at time t in the future is determined by todays discount D_0(u). It is easier to think in terms of realized returns. One unit today pays R_0(u) at time u. We can also invest one unit today and recieve R_0(t) at time t, then reinvest that at time t to get R_0(t)R_t(u) at time u. This shows R_0(u) = R_0(t)R_t(u). In terms of discounts 1/D_0(u) = 1/D_0(t)D_t(u) so D_t(u) = D_0(u)/D_0(t).
