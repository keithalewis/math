Simple Universal Model

Based on Stephen Ross's 1978 paper "A Simple Approach to the Valuation of Risky Streams"

If there are no arbitrage opportunities in the capital markets, then there exists a (not generally unique) [linear] valuation operator, L.
Variable Description
\(T\)market trading times
\(I\)all market instruments
\(\Omega\) sample space of possible outcomes
\(\mathcal{A}_t\) partition of \(\Omega\) representing information at time \(t\in T\)

Market

Instruments have prices and pay associated cash flows: bonds pay coupons, stocks pay dividends, futures have margin adjustments.

\(X_t,C_t\colon\mathcal{A}_t\to ℝ^I\)

Trading

Increasing (stopping) times \(\tau_0 < \cdots < \tau_n\) and number of shares to trade at \(\tau_j\), \(\Gamma_j\colon\mathcal{A}_{\tau_j}\to ℝ^I\)

Accounting

\(V_t = (\Delta_t + \Gamma_t)\cdot X_t\) is the value, or mark-to-market, of the portfolio.

\(A_t = \Delta_t\cdot C_t - \Gamma_t\cdot X_t\) is the amount showing up at time \(t\) in the trading account.

Arbitrage

A (finite) trading strategy \((\tau_j, \Gamma_j\)) with $A_{\tau_0} > 0$, \(A_t \ge0\), \(t > \tau_0\), and \(\sum_j \Gamma_j = 0\).

Fundamental Theorem of Asset Pricing

No arbitrage implies there exist positive measures \(D_t\) on \(\mathcal{A}_t\) with \[X_t D_t = \sum_{u > t} C_s D_s \mid {\mathcal{A}_t}\] Every model is parameterized by a vector valued martingal measure \(M_t\colon\mathcal{A}_t\to ℝ^I\) (i.e., \(M_t = M_u\mid\mathcal{A}_t\) if \(t \le u\)) and positive measures \(D_t\colon\mathcal{A}_t\to (0,\infty)\) where \[ X_t D_t = M_t - \sum_{s\le t}C_s D_s.\]

Black-Scholes/Merton

The B-S/M model takes \(T = [0,\infty)\), \(I\) is a bond and a stock, \(\Omega = C[0,\infty)\) is the set of all continuous functions on the non-negative real numbers, and \((\mathcal{A}_t)\) is the standard filtration for Brownian motion \((B_t)\) with Weiner measure \(P\).

The B-S/M model is \(M_t = (r, se^{\sigma B_t - \sigma^2 t/2})P\) and \(D_t = e^{-\rho t}P\).

Using the definitions of value and amount we have for any trading strategy \[V_t D_t = \sum_{u > t} A_s D_s \mid {\mathcal{A}_t}\] Trading creates synthetic market instruments where value corresponds to price and amount corresponds to cash flow.

Derivative

A (cash settled) derivative is a contact specifying times \((\hat{\tau}_k)\) and amounts \((\hat{A}_k)\) to be paid at that time. If a trading strategy \(\tau_j, \Gamma_j\) exists with $A_{\hat{\tau_k}} = \hat{A}_k$ and \(A_t = 0\) otherwise (self-financing), then the strategy is a perfect hedge.

Note \(V_0 D_0 = \sum_{u > 0} \hat{A}_{\hat{\tau}_k} D_{\hat{\tau}_k}\) can be computed using the contract terms \((\hat{\tau}_k,\hat{A}_k)\) and deflators \(D_t\). We also have \(V_0 = (\Delta_0 + \Gamma_0)\cdot X_0 = \Gamma_0\cdot X_0\) since the initial position $\Delta_0 = 0$. The derivative of \(V_0\) with respect to \(X_0\) is \(D_{X_0} V_0 = \Gamma_0\), so this gives the initial hedge.

In general $V_t = (\Delta_t + \Gamma_t)\cdot X_t$ so \(D_{X_t}V_t = \Delta_t + \Gamma_t\) and \(\Gamma_t = D_{X_t}V_t - \Delta_t\). Since \(\Delta_t\) is known at time $t$, this gives a general formula of computing hedges.

Black-Scholes and Merton make the untenable assumption is it is possible to hedge in continuous time. The Simple Universal Model forces the implementer to face the difficult but important question of when do you hedge?
